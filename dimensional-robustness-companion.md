# Dimensional Robustness Across Alternative Moral Psychologies
## Companion Document to "Beyond WEIRD Defaults"
### March 17, 2026

## Purpose

The main paper derives four structural dimensions (moral agent constitution, authority legitimacy, moral domain boundary, scope of moral obligation) from Haidt's Moral Foundations Theory and Darwall's second-person standpoint theory. Critics contest MFT's foundation taxonomy, arguing that the number and boundaries of foundations are underdetermined. This companion document demonstrates that the same four dimensions emerge from three alternative decompositions of moral psychology, providing evidence that they describe structural features of moral frameworks rather than artifacts of any particular theory.

The claim is not that these mappings are the only possible ones. Alternative mappings are plausible. The claim is that the four dimensions are *recoverable* across multiple independent theoretical frameworks, which is evidence (not proof) that they track something structural about how moral frameworks vary rather than something idiosyncratic about how Haidt categorizes moral intuitions.

## Shweder's Autonomy-Community-Divinity (CAD) Triad

Shweder et al. (1997) argue that moral discourse across cultures organizes around three ethics: autonomy (individual rights and harm), community (duty, hierarchy, and role obligations), and divinity (purity, sanctity, and the sacred order).

**Moral agent constitution** corresponds to the autonomy-community axis. The ethic of autonomy constitutes the moral subject as the rights-bearing individual: morality protects individual choice and prevents harm. The ethic of community constitutes the moral subject as the person-in-role: morality sustains the social order and the relationships that define identity. These are not different emphases on the same subject but different subjects. A person reasoning from the autonomy ethic asks "what are my rights?" A person reasoning from the community ethic asks "what does my role require?" The question itself is constituted differently.

**Authority legitimacy** falls within the community ethic, which treats hierarchy as morally constitutive rather than contingent. In the autonomy ethic, authority is at best instrumentally justified and at worst a threat to individual freedom. In the community ethic, social hierarchy is part of the moral order, and fulfilling one's role within that hierarchy is a moral act. The dimension captures this structural disagreement: not whether authority can be abused (all frameworks agree it can), but whether authority carries inherent moral legitimacy.

**Moral domain boundary** maps to divinity. The ethic of divinity extends moral authority to domains that the autonomy ethic treats as morally neutral: bodily practice, purity, ritual obligation, and the sacred. This is the same WEIRD blind spot the main text identifies through MFT's sanctity/degradation foundation. WEIRD populations operating primarily within the autonomy ethic do not recognize divinity-domain questions as moral questions at all. They experience these questions as matters of personal preference, tradition, or superstition. A model trained on autonomy-ethic signal inherits this blindness: it will not give wrong answers to purity questions, because it will not recognize them as the kind of question that has a moral answer.

**Scope of moral obligation** cuts across all three ethics but is most visible in the community ethic's extension of obligation to kin networks, ancestral obligations, and temporal horizons that extend beyond the individual lifespan. The autonomy ethic tends to bound obligation at the individual and the contractual. The community ethic extends it through kinship and role. The divinity ethic extends it through sacred obligation to past and future. These extensions are not additions to a common base; they reflect structurally different answers to who has standing to make moral claims.

## Gray and Schein's Theory of Dyadic Morality (TDM)

Gray and Schein (2012) argue that all moral judgment reduces to a perceived dyad: an intentional agent causing harm (or benefit) to a vulnerable patient. Moral diversity arises from variation in who counts as agent or patient, what counts as harm, and how broadly the dyadic template applies.

This reductionist framework might seem to collapse the four dimensions into one. It does not, because it reproduces all four through different mechanisms.

**Moral agent constitution** maps to variation in agent perception. TDM acknowledges that cultures differ in whether they perceive individual agents or collective agents. When a family patriarch makes a decision, some cultures perceive a single moral agent (the patriarch); others perceive a collective agent (the family acting through its designated representative). The dyadic template is preserved, but the entity occupying the "agent" position varies. This variation is the moral agent constitution dimension expressed in TDM's terms.

**Authority legitimacy** maps to whether authority figures are perceived as agents of harm (the skeptical frame) or agents of protection (the deferential frame). In TDM's structure, the same authority action can be moralized in opposite directions: a parent disciplining a child is perceived as an agent causing harm (WEIRD/autonomy frame) or an agent protecting the child from future harm (community/authority frame). The frameworks disagree not on whether the dyad applies but on which role the authority figure occupies within it.

**Moral domain boundary** is where TDM is most revealing. Gray and Schein's concept of "dyadic completion" describes the tendency to perceive a victim even where none is obvious. This is the mechanism by which some cultures moralize purity and others do not. When a person eats forbidden food in private, one culture completes the dyad (the person's soul is harmed, or the sacred order is violated, producing a "victim") and perceives a moral violation. Another culture does not complete the dyad (no victim, no moral question) and perceives a private choice. The frameworks do not disagree about the moral answer. They disagree about whether a moral question exists. Dyadic completion is the TDM mechanism that generates the moral domain boundary dimension.

**Scope of moral obligation** maps to variation in patient perception: who is vulnerable enough to warrant moral concern, and across what distance and time. Cultures that extend moral patient status to ancestors, future generations, or the natural world draw the scope of obligation wider than cultures that restrict patient status to currently living humans in proximate relationships. The Moral Machine experiment's finding of systematic cross-cultural variation in whose welfare weighs in moral calculations is a direct measurement of this variation in patient perception.

## Curry's Morality-as-Cooperation (MAC)

Curry et al. (2019) derive seven moral domains from distinct cooperation problems: family values (kin altruism), group loyalty (mutualism), reciprocity (exchange), bravery (conflict resolution), respect (deference hierarchies), fairness (division), and property rights (possession). Testing across 60 societies, they find that all seven are recognized as morally relevant everywhere, though the weighting varies.

This is the most granular decomposition and might appear to multiply the structural picture. But the four dimensions organize MAC's seven domains into coherent clusters.

**Moral agent constitution** corresponds to the tension between family values and group loyalty (which constitute the moral agent as relational, embedded in kin and community structures) versus property rights and fairness (which constitute the moral agent as an individual with personal entitlements and claims). Both clusters are morally relevant in all cultures; the structural question is which cluster takes priority when they conflict. A dispute over inheritance, for example, activates family values and property rights simultaneously. Whether the claim of the eldest son (relational) or the claim of equal division (individual) takes precedence depends on how the moral agent is constituted.

**Authority legitimacy** maps directly to Curry's respect domain: whether deference hierarchies carry inherent moral weight. This is the most straightforward mapping across all four theories. The structural question is whether hierarchical position, independent of the person occupying it, generates moral obligations. Cultures that weight the respect domain heavily answer yes; cultures that weight it weakly answer no.

**Moral domain boundary** is less explicit in MAC because Curry's cooperation framework already treats all seven domains as moral. However, the framework predicts that populations emphasizing different cooperation problems will draw the moral boundary differently. A population that heavily weights purity norms (which MAC might subsume under group loyalty or a separate sanctity-related cooperation problem, depending on interpretation) will moralize domains that a population emphasizing fairness and property rights treats as outside morality. The structural prediction is the same: where the boundary of "moral" sits varies with the cooperation problems a culture prioritizes.

**Scope of moral obligation** maps to the reach of reciprocity and group loyalty: how far the cooperative circle extends and who falls within it. Cultures with strong kin altruism and group loyalty extend moral obligation to extended family and community. Cultures with strong reciprocity and fairness norms may extend it more broadly (to anyone within the exchange network) or more narrowly (only to those who reciprocate). The temporal dimension (obligation to ancestors and descendants) maps to the inter-generational extension of kin altruism.

## Implications for the Rationalization Layer

The four dimensions survive all three alternative decompositions. This robustness obtains because the dimensions describe the structural axes along which moral frameworks vary, not the content-level triggers any particular theory identifies. Each theory provides a different explanation for *why* the dimensions exist (different ethics, different dyadic perceptions, different cooperation problems), but all three locate the same axes of variation.

The practical implication is that the rationalization layer's decision procedure does not depend on MFT being correct. If MFT's specific foundation taxonomy were replaced by Shweder's triad, Gray and Schein's dyadic template, or Curry's cooperation domains as the content-level theory, the four structural dimensions and the two-stage classification process (distributional convergence via EMD, then reasoning analysis) would remain unchanged.

This does not mean the four dimensions are necessarily complete. A dimension might exist that all four theories miss because it falls outside their shared assumptions. Epistemic source (where moral truth originates) is a candidate: it appears in none of these theories' primary decompositions but may vary independently of the dimensions they predict. The architecture accommodates dimensional revision without structural change, because EMD computation, narrative clustering, and three-category classification all operate per-dimension independently.
